Child Slavery and Forced Labour

Understanding exploitation in global supply chains and what can be done to protect vulnerable workers and children.

What Is Forced Labour?

Forced labour occurs when people are made to work against their will under threat of punishment. This punishment does not always involve physical violence, it can also include withheld wages, debt bondage, threats to family members, confiscation of identity documents, or restrictions on movement.

Forced labour exists in many industries worldwide, including agriculture, manufacturing, mining, construction, domestic work, and fishing. Because global supply chains are complex and often cross multiple countries, forced labour can remain hidden from both governments and consumers.

What Is Child Labour?

Child labour refers to work that deprives children of their childhood, education, health, or dignity. Not all work done by children is considered harmful, but child labour becomes a serious issue when it interferes with schooling or exposes children to dangerous or exploitative conditions.

Children are particularly vulnerable to exploitation due to poverty, lack of access to education, displacement, and weak legal protections in some regions.

What Is Child Slavery?

Child slavery is an extreme form of child exploitation. It occurs when children are forced to work under conditions they cannot escape, often through coercion, threats, or control by others. Children in slavery may be denied freedom, education, and basic rights, and are unable to leave their situation safely.

Child slavery is commonly linked to forced labour but can also involve other forms of exploitation, including trafficking.

Why Forced Labour and Child Slavery Persist

Several factors contribute to the continued existence of forced labour and child slavery:

Poverty and Economic Inequality

Lack of economic opportunities makes people vulnerable to exploitation.

Limited Access to Education

Without education, children have fewer opportunities and protections.

Weak Labour Law Enforcement

Insufficient oversight allows unethical practices to continue.

Conflict and Displacement

Vulnerable populations in crisis situations face higher exploitation risks.

Hidden Supply Chains

Complex supplier networks make it difficult to track where products are made.

Insufficient Oversight

Unethical practices remain hidden due to lack of transparency and monitoring.

Global Impact

Millions of people worldwide are affected by forced labour and child exploitation. These practices generate significant illegal profits while denying workers and children their basic rights and opportunities. The impact extends beyond individuals, reinforcing cycles of poverty and inequality within communities.

Why Consumer Awareness Matters

Many consumers are unaware that everyday products can be linked to forced labour or child exploitation. Increasing awareness helps create demand for transparency and ethical sourcing. When consumers ask questions and support responsible companies, businesses face greater pressure to improve labour practices.

Awareness is a critical step toward accountability and change.

How Forced Labour Shows Up in Everyday Products

Here are common product categories where forced labour or child labour risks are known to appear.

Clothing & Textiles

Raw cotton and denim production often involves seasonal work and subcontracted farms where oversight is limited.

Why this matters: Garment value chains are long and opaque, hiding labour risk upstream.

Electronics

Mining for minerals and battery production can involve hazardous conditions and informal labour arrangements.

Why this matters: Small parts and components come from many suppliers, increasing risk.

Seafood

Fishing fleets and processing plants sometimes rely on coerced or debt-bonded workers at sea.

Why this matters: Offshore work is hard to monitor and workers can be isolated.

Chocolate & Coffee

Agricultural harvesting and smallholder farms are high-risk settings for child labour and exploitation.

Why this matters: Seasonal harvests and informal labour make protections inconsistent.

Mining & Raw Materials

Artisanal mining and subcontracted supply chains can involve children and forced labour for high-demand minerals.

Why this matters: Minerals feed many industries, making the problem widespread.

A Product's Journey

Risks can enter products at multiple steps along the supply chain.

1. Raw Materials

Forced labour is often hidden in agriculture and mining, far from consumer visibility.

2. Manufacturing

Subcontracting and informal factories can create gaps in oversight and worker protections.

3. Assembly

Assembly plants may use temporary or agency labour with weak contracts and limited rights.

4. Shipping & Retail

Long logistics chains and multiple intermediaries make tracing labour conditions difficult.

Why It's Hard to See

Several structural issues make forced labour difficult to detect and address.

  • Complex supply chains with many tiers
  • Outsourcing and subcontracting that hide true employers
  • Weak enforcement and uneven labour inspections
  • Lack of clear product labelling and supplier disclosure

What Consumers Can (and Can't) Control

Consumers Can

  • Ask questions about sourcing
  • Support companies that publish supply chains
  • Use tools to spot risks and demand transparency
  • Apply public pressure through choices and advocacy

Consumers Can't

  • See every tier of a supply chain alone
  • Audit factories or enforce labour law
  • Guarantee a product is 100% free of risk

Frequently Asked Questions

Is every product made with forced labour?

No. Risk varies by product, supplier and region. Our tool highlights where risks have been reported so you can investigate further.

Does "ethical" mean perfect?

Not necessarily. Ethical sourcing reduces risk but does not guarantee perfection. Transparency and continuous improvement matter.

Why don't companies disclose this information?

Disclosure can be costly and complex. Many companies lack oversight of deeper supplier tiers or fear reputational risk.

What Can Be Done

Addressing forced labour and child slavery requires cooperation between governments, businesses, and consumers. Key actions include:

  • ✔ Stronger labour laws and enforcement
  • ✔ Greater supply chain transparency
  • ✔ Independent monitoring and reporting
  • ✔ Corporate accountability
  • ✔ Informed consumer choices
